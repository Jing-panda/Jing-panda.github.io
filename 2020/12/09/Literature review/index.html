Literature overview

Title: The value of early-stage phenotyping for wheat breeding in the age of genomic selection

Author: Borrenpohl Daniel, Huang Mao, Olson Eric, Sneller Clay

Institute: Department of Horticulture and Crop Science, Ohio Agriculture Research and Development Center, The Ohio State University, USA

Journal: Theor Appl Genet

Date: 01 June 2020

DOI: https://doi.org/10.1007/s00122-020-03613-0

Introduction

Genomic selection

Genomic selection was first proposed by Meuwissen et al. (2001).

Theodorus Meuwissen is a professor at Norwegian University of Life Sciences, in the area of animal genetics.

Here is a video of his presentation about genomic selection:

Summary from presentation of Meuwissen

Why MAS (marker assistant selection) not the solution

  • Traits of interest are complex with many genes controlled, and affected by environment

  • Detection of QTL (quantitative traits loci) explain only small fraction of genetic variation

Three breakthroughs he mentioned

  • Detection of large number of SNP marker— Byproduct of sequencing efforts

  • SNP-chip genotyping technology — Affordable to genotype 50, 000 SNPs

  • Development of GS/ GP technology

Some key features of GS

  • SNP effects are not tested for statistical significance — all SNP effects are used $\geqslant$ all genetic variance are addressed

  • Accurate GEBV for individuals without records — decouples elite breeding from recording

    • Invasive recordings
    • Late in life records
    • Sex limited traits

  • No pedigree recoding needed, because pedigree is replaced by marker based relationships

Statistic model

  • linear regression: SNP-BLUP $\equiv$ GBLUP

    $$
    y_i= \mu+X_{1x}* b_1 + X_{2i}* b_2 + … + X_{5000000} * b_{50000} + e_i
    $$

    $$
    GEBV_j= X_{1j}* b_1 + X_{2j} * b_2 + … + X_{500000j}*b_{500000}
    $$

    ⭐all SNPs are same, and have the same constant variation, with mean of 0.

  • non-linear regression -Bayes A, Bayes B, Bayes C

    $$
    y_i = I_1 * X_{i1}* b_1 + I_2 * X_{i2} * b_2 + … + I_{500000} * X_{i500000} * b_{500000} + e_i
    $$

    • $I_j=0/1$ is an indicator whether SNP j has effect or not

    • Prior information is that fraction $\pi$ of SNPs have effect & $(1-\pi)$ have no effect

    • Try to give extra weight to some SNPs and less to others

Simulation studies: GBLUP < Bayes A <Bayes C

Real data studies show:

  • Differences small
  • often GBLUP is as good as BayesA/B

Theoretically:

  • If few and dense SNPs, BayesB is best
  • If many genes / not enough density: GBLUP best

Precision breeding (Flint & Woolliams, 2007)

  1. Accurate GEBV
  2. Avoid deleterious side effects from breeding — select for broad breeding goal
  3. Manage genetic variation — Realize precision breeding also into the future

In GS, a training population (TP) is formed if individuals who are phenotyped and genotyped with molecular markers. Those data are co-analyzed to build a prediction model.

The model can then be used to predict the trait value of unphenotyped individuals that are related to the TP and have been genotyped with the same markers.

The GS model calculates genomic estimated breeding values (GEBVs) for the unphenotyped individuals that can be used for selection in the same manner as phenotypic data.

Application of GS in plant breeding

Population improvement phase

The first application is for the population improvement phase of a program where recombination creates new genetic variation and testable progeny are generated. GS in this phase can increase the rate of genetic gain by reducing the duration of a recurrent selection breeding cycle.

Product development phase — field testing

GS can also be applied to the product development phase of breeding where progeny are evaluated for performance in a stage-gate process. GS could enhance field trial selection by integrating genotypic and phenotypic data as opposed to advancing lines using phenotypic data alone.

Obejectives

  1. Compare the prediction accuracy of GS and phenotyping in stage-1 and stage-2 field evaluations
  2. Assess the value of stage-1 phenotyping for advancing lines to stage-2 testing

Materials and methods

Training population: 1769 wheat breeding lines (genotyped +phenotypes)

Traits: yield, test weight, Fusarium head blight resistance, heading date, and height.

SNP discovery: 400 randomly selected lines was first used for single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) discovery by comparing their sequences to the wheat reference genome in the TASSEL-GBS pipeline (Glaubitz et al. 2014). In total, ~ 190,000 SNPs were detected.

SNP cleaning: Only SNPs with less than 20% missing data and greater than 0.05 minor allele frequency were retained. Missing marker scores were imputed using an expectation–maximization algorithm in R package rrBLUP (v. 4.6) using the “A.mat” function (Endelman 2011). To ensure even marker genome coverage and reduce marker redundancy, a SNP tagging procedure using a threshold of r = 0.8 was conducted to identify the most informative markers (Rinaldo et al. 2005; Huang et al. 2016). After this tagging procedure, a total of 12,037 high-quality and evenly distributed SNPs were used for analysis.

Population structure: Population structure among all 1769 lines was assessed using principal component analysis of the marker data in the R stats package (v. 3.5.3) using the “prcomp” function (R Core Development Team 2019).

Modeling: GS models were built using R package rrBLUP (v. 4.6) to estimate marker effects for each trait using ridge regression BLUP (RR-BLUP) using the “mixed.solve” function (Endelman, 2011). RR-BLUP was chosen primarily for computational efficiency because Huang et al. (2016) found that RR-BLUP had comparable accuracy to other GS models for all wheat traits in the Ohio State University breeding program.

Conclusion

In conclusion, GS is proving to be a valuable tool for improving the efficiency of the population improvement and product development phases of breeding field trials. Introducing GS into a program can have a dramatic benefit to a breeding program. But applying GS requires a thorough assessment of the impact of GS on genetic gain, cost per unit of gain, allocation of field-testing resources, and operational considerations in all phases of breeding phases.
